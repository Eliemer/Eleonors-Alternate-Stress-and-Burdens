** Skill Checks
Performing a *Skill Check* is done by rolling a pool of d6's and
taking the result of the highest number rolled.

#+CAPTION: Success Gradient
| Highest result | Outcome          | Consequence | Effect       |
|----------------+------------------+-------------+--------------|
|            1-3 | Failure          | \check      |              |
|            4-5 | Partial Success  | \check      | \check       |
|              6 | Success          |             | \check       |
|   multiple 6's | Critical Success |             | \check\check |

*** Bonuses
Bonuses are measured using *Accuracy* and *Difficulty*. *Accuracy*
comes from various sources like *Skill Triggers*, *Position* and
*Equipment*, among whatever other sources your players can
successfully argue for. *Difficulty* largely comes from *Position*,
but may also include specific circumstances, or the level of *Harm*
the player character has when making this check, or just because you,
as the GM, might want to make the roll harder. The final d6 pool has a
minimum of 1d6, no matter how many remaining difficulties.
- Remove 1d6 per *Difficulty*
- Add 1d6 per *Accuracy*
- Acc/Diff cancel 1:1 as normal

*** Using Skill Triggers as bonuses
Instead of ~1d20 +2/+4/+6~, as you would in the core rules, roll ~[1 +
{1/2/3}] d6kh1~. That is, for each point in the relevant Skill Trigger
used, add 1d6 to the roll the highest after all bonuses have been
calculated.

** Resistance rolls
On receiving a Consequence, you may automatically ignore all of it, at
the cost of ~6 - Xd6kh1~ Stress, where ~X~ is number of relevant
skills. Regardless of how many points of each skill you may have, only
the number of relevant skills counts.

** Position and Effect
*Position* reflects how well a PC can execute their plan of
action. Attempting to sneak up behind a guard in the dead of night
with a cloaking device will provide a much higher Positioning than
attempting the same in broad daylight and directly in their light of
sight, while they're surrounded by 50 of their buddies. There are
three common *Positions*: *Controlled*, *Risky*, *Desperate*. The most
common *Position* should be *Risky*. This roughly corresponds with
normal, risky, and heroic rolls in LANCER.

#+CAPTION: Position Table
| Position   | Bonus         | Examples                                                                  |
|------------+---------------+---------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Controlled | +1 Accuracy   | No urgency, plentiful tools, good training, etc.                          |
| Risky      | 0             | Time is of the essence, a skillful attempt, dangerous environment         |
| Desperate  | +1 Difficulty | You are on the back foot, unprepared, out-skilled, in overwhelming danger |
|            |               | <30>                                                                      |
|------------+---------------+---------------------------------------------------------------------------|

*Effect* reflects how much their plan can actually solve the problem
at hand. It doesn't matter how many 6s you roll if you're trying to
blow up a capital ship with a bandage. If their plan of action has no
possibility of producing any effect whatever the result of the roll,
ask your player to come up with another plan entirely; This one won't
work. *Effect* /roughly/ translates to how many segments of the goal
clock you'd be able to fill up with a success. The most common
*Effects* are: *Limited*, *Standard*, *Great*, as well as *Zero*, and
*Extreme*. Most rolls should be *Standard Effect*.

#+CAPTION: Effects Tables
| Effect   | Segments | Outcome                                                                  |
|----------+----------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Zero     |       +0 | Nothing is achieved                                                      |
| Limited  |       +1 | Follow-up actions are needed to achieve the goal                         |
| Standard |       +2 | A simple goal is achieved, or significant progress towards a complex one |
| Great    |       +3 | Substantial progress is made towards a complex goal                      |
| Extreme  |       +4 | Most complex goals are achieved in their entirety                        |
|          |          | <40>                                                                     |
|----------+----------+--------------------------------------------------------------------------|

When asking for a roll, ask for their plan which roughly establishes
*Position*, and negotiate what would the appropriate *Effect* their plan
and *Position* would achieve. It is OK to go back and forth a short bit
to properly assess the situation

** Consequences
Skill checks need to represent a real possibility of consequence; If
nothing happens on a failure, just don't roll. Always establish the
consequences *before* the rolls happen. The most common sources of
consequences in LANCER are: *HP/Stress*, and narrative
repercussions.

For example, if you fail to persuade a noble, maybe you don't get shot
instantly, but your reputation with them is worsened. They will send a
gang of thugs to beat you up for your transgressions later on. Don't
be afraid to harm a pilot's HP directly if it makes narrative sense,
they can resist it with *Stress* using a *Resistence Roll*, give them
the option and incentive to do so.

** Burdens
When you exceed your *Stress* bar, you obtain a *Burden*, as detailed
in _Field Guide to the Karrakin Trade Baronies_. However, once you
obtain a *Burden*, you will suffer an immediate negative effect that
lasts until the end of the current *Scene*, as well as a persistent
negative effect which lasts for as long as you have /at least/ that
many *Burdens*

** Accuracies and Difficulties
These come from anything and everything, if relevant to the action at
hand.
- Background
- Pilot Gear, Mech equipments, tools, items, etc.
- Contacts
- Reputation
- etc.
